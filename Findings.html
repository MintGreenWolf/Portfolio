User Research

User Interviews

"Uniquness, confidence, experience"

In order to understand what a hiring manager would like to see in a portfolio website I conducted user interviews on friends who do hiring in creative sectors. A major theme of these interviews was uniqueness and ability to perform the job. In fact, when asked what makes a designer stand out, one respondent said: "Uniqueness, confidence, experience." Another respondent, said that: "work ethic" was important to show in a portfolio.

Feature Value Matrix

"Headshot and Email"

According to my user interviews, I found that the number one thing hiring managers look for on a portfolio is a headshot and Email contact link. These two things were even above the examples of work and resume. I guess you can't get hired if they don't know who you are and how to contact you.

User Testing

Click Test

"It literally says bio"

The click test notified me that my portfolio was easy to navigate. The test asked participants to click the user's bio. Users found this test to be quite easy as evidence by their responses. Most users described the test with comments like: "It said bio duh" etc. The heatmap, pictured left, shows a visual representation of passing clicks on the correct bio button. In fact, out of 16 responses, 18 clicks landed on the correct bio link.

Click test results can be found here

Free Response

"wut? no. call me ;)"

I presented users with a photo of my portfolio and asked them questions like; "Does the design give you a sense of who the designer is?" and "Can you easily find the designers contact info?". These questions were important as they were some of the main points I gathered from my user interviews. Although the my answers were mostly positive (See photo of the wordcloud to the left),from these I found out that my contact information is hard to find as one user said: " wut? no. call me ;)"

Cognitive Walkthrough

"Is that right?"

The cognitive walkthrough allowed me to analyze tasks and to take notes on participant anctions and their reactions. This was important as I was able to tell live in person what was hard to navigate and find in my interface and what was easy. It was interesting that sometimes participants would ask if a particular action was right. The cognitive walkthroughs really cemented my need to have a clearly labeled contact email. It was also a challenge to find willing participants and to not want to help them when they got lost.

Summary of Findings


From my user research I found that most thought my portfolio was interesting and cute. Many liked that everything was simplistic and minimal. For instance, the animations were pleasing to users who participated in my user reasearch. Many users commented that this was aesthetically pleasing. However not all was right with the design.

The design presented many flaws that led to users not understanding where to click or how to navigate it. One user said that he wasn't sure where to click to find the contact information. Should a user click my bio, my resume or both for contact information? Also the pages are sparse and I haven't had access to my desktop and this has prevented me from finding relevant references of past work I have done.

Incorporation of Findings

To increase my chances of landing a job I plan to use both the positive and negative comments and findings from my research to better my portfolio. Users were clear that the contact was hard to find, the resume and the bio could be confusing and the text inside of the circular links should be on the outside of the circles. Besides these fixes, I will include a contact button, more examples of work, and to clean up the code.

I would also like to continue building the portfolio and making the site itself an example of my work. For example, I am currently working on getting my website to be even more responsive and to include more interesting javascript and user interactions.

In the future I hope that my portfolio will be a onestop shop as a peice of design in itself. And, of course, to get a job with it.
